# FLO Landing Page — Benchmarks & Compliance Brief

_Compiled April 2026. Scope: a ~$130 filtered shower head sold DTC from cold paid traffic (Meta, Google, TikTok), with a Subscribe & Save filter replacement motion._

---

## Part 1 — Conversion Benchmarks

### Cold-traffic CVR baseline (DTC beauty/wellness, ~$100–150 AOV)

Three independent data sets converge on a similar window for a landing page — not a PDP — sitting behind a paid-social cold-traffic ad:

| Traffic source | Realistic CVR range | Top-quartile |
|---|---|---|
| Meta (cold) → dedicated LP | **1.5 – 2.5%** | 2.5 – 4%+ |
| TikTok (cold) → dedicated LP | 1.0 – 2.0% | 2.5 – 3.5% |
| Google Search (branded/high-intent) | 3 – 6% | 8%+ |
| Google Shopping / PMax (cold) | 1.5 – 3% | 4%+ |

Cold social to a **standard Shopify PDP** typically runs 0.5 – 1.5% — this is the number most brands quietly hit and complain about. A dedicated LP with message-match, founder presence, and heavy social proof is what moves you into the 2%+ band. (MHI Growth Engine; Shopify; Madgicx.)

Category-wide, Shopify's own data puts **Health & Beauty site-wide CVR at 2.5 – 5.7%** and **Healthcare & Wellness at ~3.1% average** — but that's a blend of branded traffic, returning visitors, and email, so don't plan to cold-traffic at that number. (Clean Commit; Blend Commerce.)

### Warm / retargeting CVR

- Retargeting + email / SMS warm audiences: **4 – 8% CVR** on the same LP is standard; top performers 8 – 12%.
- Subscribe-specific conversion on **cold** traffic: **0.5 – 2%**. On warm/returning users: **up to ~10%**. (Recharge.)

### Jolie benchmark (the comp that matters)

Jolie went from ~$4M to $28M in two years and is targeting $40M+ in 2025, tracking to $100M by 2027. The mechanics worth stealing:

- **Subscribe-driven LTV, not CVR-driven acquisition.** $148 hardware + $33 filter every 90 days. Filter subscriber churn is <1%/month; some cohorts 99% retention at month 5. Subscriber base = ~$10M ARR. (Beauty Independent; Founder Secrets; Smartrr case study.)
- **Paid marketing is not the growth engine.** Babenzien has publicly said "paid marketing just isn't working" and leaned into influencer seeding + OOH. 25,000+ UGC pieces in ~2 years. (Modern Retail; Marketing Brew.)
- **Implication for FLO:** if you're building a paid-acquisition LP, you're solving a problem Jolie stopped trying to solve with paid. Expect paid CVR in the 1.5–2.5% band but plan the P&L around sub retention, not first-order ROAS.

### ROAS benchmarks (paid)

| Platform | Average | Beauty/personal care | Healthy target for ~$130 AOV |
|---|---|---|---|
| Meta (all) | 2.19x | Below-median; CPM $10.88 Q1'25 | **2.5 – 3.0x blended** |
| TikTok | varies | **3.5x** (Beauty & Personal Care leads) | 2.8 – 4.0x |
| Google | 3 – 5x | — | 4x+ |

Rule of thumb: for impulse/lifestyle beauty, **3.0+ ROAS is "strong."** Blended MER for beauty brands ran ~0.59 in 2025. (Varos; Triple Whale; Upcounting.)

### Subscribe & Save AOV lift

- Sub conversion on PDP/LP: typically 20–45% of buyers in wellness when the default is selected and discount is 10–15%.
- AOV lift on first order from sub vs one-time: often neutral to slightly lower (discount offsets), but **90-day revenue per acquired customer is typically 40–80% higher** for subscribers.
- Best practice: default-selected subscription with prepaid-3-pack upsell — drives AOV 15–30% higher than single-filter default.

### Shopify PDP vs dedicated LP delta

Dedicated LPs convert **2–5x better than standard PDPs** for paid traffic. Removing nav alone is +5–15%. Well-optimized LPs hit 5–15% CVR on qualified traffic; 6.6% is considered the "good" ecomm LP benchmark. For any single campaign spending $1K+/month, a dedicated LP is almost always ROI-positive. (Shopify; Apexure; DTC Pages.)

**Planning numbers for FLO paid traffic to a new dedicated LP:**
- Month 1 baseline: **1.5 – 2.2% CVR** cold Meta/TikTok
- Optimized (8–12 weeks): **2.5 – 3.5%** cold
- Retargeting: **5 – 8%**
- Target blended ROAS: **2.5x** to break even on CAC assuming ~60% sub attach and 3+ filter cycles in LTV

## Part 2 — Filtration Tech Cheat Sheet

**KDF-55** — copper-zinc redox media. The workhorse for chlorine in cold/warm water. Independently reduces **free chlorine by ~85–95%** via electrochemical conversion to chloride. Also knocks down water-soluble heavy metals (lead, mercury) at low levels. **Does not remove chloramine effectively, does not soften water.** Performance drops as flow rate and temperature rise. Industry-standard language: "chlorine reduction via redox media."

**KDF-85** — similar chemistry tuned for iron and hydrogen sulfide ("rotten egg" smell). Rare in shower heads; more common in whole-house systems. Mention only if actually used.

**Calcium sulfite** — neutralizes free chlorine via reduction reaction. Unlike KDF, **maintains ~90%+ effectiveness at hot-shower temperatures (up to ~110°F)**, which is why premium shower filters pair it with KDF (cold-side reduction) for a dual-media stack. Also ineffective on chloramine.

**Activated carbon (GAC or coconut-shell block)** — adsorption media. Great for taste/odor, VOCs, and some pesticides in cold-contact drinking filters, but in a shower head the **contact time is too short (<1 second) for meaningful removal at hot temperatures**. Don't lead with carbon claims for a shower product; it's a supporting media, not the hero. Marketing claim language should be conservative: "helps reduce odor" rather than "removes VOCs."

**Vitamin C (ascorbic / sodium ascorbate)** — chemically neutralizes both **chlorine AND chloramine** at ~99%+ regardless of temperature. This is the only common media that handles chloramine at shower-contact times. Downside: consumable (depletes faster than KDF), and the media has a defined capacity measured in gallons. If FLO targets chloraminated municipalities (much of California, Denver, DC, Philadelphia), vitamin C is a meaningful differentiator. (PristineHydro; Pickiniki; Second Shower.)

**The chloramine caveat that matters:** NSF/ANSI 177 **only tests free chlorine reduction, not chloramine**. A product can be "NSF 177 certified" and still do essentially nothing in a chloramine city. If FLO serves chloramine markets, say so explicitly and back it with lab data — don't hide behind 177. (Plumbing & Mechanical mag; Pickiniki.)

**What shower filters cannot do (ever):**
- **Soften hard water.** True softening requires ion-exchange resin + regeneration with salt — physically incompatible with a shower-head form factor. Any "softens water" claim is FTC-risky; most competitors use weasel language like "conditioning" which is also thin ice.
- **Remove fluoride, nitrates, or TDS** meaningfully at shower contact times.
- **Kill bacteria / viruses.** Don't imply it.

## Part 3 — Claim Matrix

| SAFE to say (on-brand, defensible) | Needs proof, rephrase, or cut |
|---|---|
| "Reduces chlorine by up to [X]% per NSF/ANSI 177 testing." (X = your actual tested number) | "Removes chlorine." — absolute; avoid without 100% data |
| "Designed to reduce chlorine, odor, and sediment." | "Purifies your water." — implies potability / health |
| "Many users report softer-feeling hair and less dry skin after switching." (with real review sourcing) | "Clinically proven to stop hair breakage." — needs RCT evidence |
| "Ingredients like KDF-55 and calcium sulfite help neutralize free chlorine." | "Detoxifies your shower." — "detox" is an FTC magnet |
| "May help reduce the drying effects of chlorinated shower water." | "Cures eczema" / "treats dermatitis" — drug claim, FDA territory |
| "Filter media independently tested to reduce free chlorine." | "Removes heavy metals and toxins." — vague + unsubstantiated |
| "Softer-feeling water." (perceptual, not chemical) | "Softens hard water." — physically false for this form factor |
| "Third-party lab tested to [standard]." (cite lab + report) | "Scientifically proven." — triggers FTC "competent and reliable scientific evidence" standard |
| "Replace filter every 3 months or 10,000 gallons." | "Lasts a lifetime." — capacity claims need data |
| "Compatible with chloraminated water — tested to reduce chloramine by up to [X]%." (only if vitamin C media and you have the test) | "Works on every water type." — too broad |
| "Featured in [publication]." | "Dermatologist-recommended." — needs named derms, documented recommendations |

Key FTC principle: **qualifiers like "may help" and "helps" do NOT by themselves rescue an unsubstantiated claim.** They reduce but don't eliminate risk. Substantiation obligation scales with the claim's implied specificity. "Helps reduce chlorine" with NSF 177 in the room is fine. "Helps reduce hair breakage" with nothing but testimonials is not. (FTC Health Products Guidance 2022; Covington; Davis Wright Tremaine.)

## Part 4 — Certification Guide (where each one goes)

| Cert | What it proves | Where it lives on the LP |
|---|---|---|
| **NSF/ANSI 177** | ≥50% free chlorine reduction over rated capacity; material safety; structural integrity; flow rate | Hero badge if you have it. Also tech/science section with the actual % and gallons figures. Link to the NSF listing page. |
| **NSF/ANSI 42** | Aesthetic effects (chlorine, taste, odor) — broader cousin to 177 | Secondary trust bar. If you have 42 but not 177, lead with 42 + explain it. |
| **NSF/ANSI 61** | Materials don't leach contaminants into water | Footer / FAQ / spec sheet. Low prominence but meaningful for trust. |
| **NSF/ANSI 372** | "Lead-free" per Safe Drinking Water Act (≤0.25% wetted lead) | Spec section + Prop 65 response. Pairs with "lead-free brass." |
| **IAPMO** | Alternative certifier to NSF; equivalent weight for 177/372 | Same placement as NSF. Say "NSF or IAPMO certified to [standard]" — don't blend logos deceptively. |

**If FLO does not have NSF 177 certification yet:**
- DO NOT use the NSF logo, the letters "NSF," or the number 177 anywhere on the site.
- DO say: "Tested by [independent lab name] to reduce free chlorine by [X]% over [Y] gallons" — with the actual report available on request.
- DO describe the media ("KDF-55 + calcium sulfite") since ingredient disclosure is not a certification claim.
- DO NOT say "NSF-grade," "meets NSF standards," or "independently certified" without naming the certifier. All three get brands into trouble.
- Roadmap: getting to NSF 177 takes ~4–6 months and ~$15–25K in testing fees. It's worth it if paid media is the core channel — the badge earns its cost back in CVR lift on skeptics.

## Part 5 — Red-Flag Phrases to Avoid

Cut these from copy, alt text, meta descriptions, ad creative, and email:

**Health-claim landmines (FDA drug-claim territory):**
- "Cures / treats / heals [eczema, psoriasis, dandruff, acne, hair loss]"
- "Anti-inflammatory"
- "Medical-grade"
- "Therapeutic"
- "Prevents [any disease or condition]"

**FTC-substantiation landmines:**
- "Detox" / "detoxifies" / "pulls toxins"
- "Removes 99.9% of contaminants" (without the exact contaminant list + certified data)
- "Purifies water" (implies potable; you're a shower filter, not a point-of-use drinking filter)
- "Clinically proven" (without published RCT)
- "Dermatologist-recommended" (without named derms + documented process)
- "Scientifically shown to reverse hair damage"
- "Spa-grade mineralization" / "molecular ionization" / "quantum water structuring" — pseudoscience tells, and the FTC reads copy

**Category-specific landmines for shower filters:**
- "Softens hard water" — physically false at this form factor. FTC complaints in the water-filtration category cluster around this.
- "Eliminates chloramine" without vitamin C media AND test data
- "Removes heavy metals" without specifying which metals, at what inlet concentration, and citing a test
- "Anti-aging shower" — beauty-claim stack that implies skin remodeling

**Safe rewrites for the above:**
- Instead of "softens" → "softer-feeling water" or "conditions feel"
- Instead of "detoxes your shower" → "reduces chlorine and chloramine in your shower water"
- Instead of "cures dry skin" → "users tell us their skin feels less tight after showering"
- Instead of "removes heavy metals" → "KDF-55 is designed to reduce dissolved metals including lead at typical municipal levels"

**Proposition 65 (California):** Most shower heads contain brass components, which contain trace lead. California requires a Prop 65 warning even when lead levels are federally "lead-free" (i.e., meeting NSF 372). **Include a Prop 65 notice on the product page, cart, and shipping confirmation for CA orders.** Standard language:

> **⚠️ WARNING:** This product can expose you to chemicals including lead, which is known to the State of California to cause cancer and birth defects or other reproductive harm. For more information go to www.P65Warnings.ca.gov.

The warning does NOT mean the product is unsafe — NSF 372 / SDWA lead-free brass is already below federal thresholds. Prop 65 is a disclosure law, not a safety standard. Skipping it on a product sold in California is a bounty-hunter lawsuit magnet.
